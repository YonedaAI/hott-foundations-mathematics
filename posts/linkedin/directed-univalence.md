Voevodsky's Univalence Axiom — the cornerstone of Homotopy Type Theory — states that equivalent types are identical. It is a profoundly symmetric principle: identities have inverses, and the universe of types behaves as an ∞-groupoid. But most mathematical structures of interest are not symmetric. Categories have morphisms that point in one direction. Representations are covariant or contravariant but not both simultaneously. The Langlands correspondence is a directed relationship. If type theory is to serve as a genuine internal language for modern mathematics, it needs a directed analogue of univalence.

The search for directed univalence has been underway since Riehl and Shulman introduced simplicial type theory (STT) in 2017. STT provides a synthetic theory of (∞,1)-categories in which hom-types Hom_A(a,b) are first-class entities, and it proved a partial directed univalence result: covariant families over A are classified by functors A → S_disc into the discrete sub-universe, with the family data recoverable up to equivalence from the functor. For seven years, whether the directed universe itself satisfied a complete directed univalence principle remained open.

A new paper, "Directed Univalence: From Riehl-Shulman to a Complete Principle," provides the most comprehensive account yet of where this programme stands. It presents the Gratzer-Weinberger-Buchholtz (2024) result — the first complete directed univalence theorem, proving Hom_S(A,B) ≃ Fun(A,B) for the universe S of discrete types in their triangulated type theory — and then carefully analyses four candidate principles of increasing strength for the full universe. The obstructions are made precise: modal dependence, opposite-type coherence, and the absence of an internal universe of (∞,1)-categories.

This is Part V of Volume II of the Univalent Correspondence series. Directed univalence is the structural prerequisite for a synthetic treatment of representation theory inside type theory — which is the route through which the Langlands programme may eventually interface with HoTT-native analytic number theory, the overarching goal of this series.
