Did you know that the digits of π can be described as the UNIQUE stream satisfying a simple observational rule — no calculus required?

A new research paper from the YonedaAI Collaboration explores what happens when you flip the usual approach to defining π and e upside down.

The usual way mathematicians define π involves limits: you sum up a series, or you describe the smallest positive zero of the sine function, and you check that the sequence of approximations converges. This is the "inductive" approach — building a real number from below, piece by piece.

The new paper asks: what if instead you describe π from the OUTSIDE? What if you say "π is the unique infinite digit stream whose structure satisfies this observable rule," and prove that exactly one such stream exists?

In mathematics this is called a coinductive or "final coalgebra" approach. Instead of constructing π from pieces, you characterise it by how it BEHAVES — what digits it emits, and what rule governs them. The paper proves, inside Homotopy Type Theory, that both approaches give the same π and the same e, and that each is genuinely the ONLY element satisfying its rule.

This matters because the two approaches have different computational properties. The inductive approach is good at proving things. The coinductive approach is good at computing things — generating digits one by one with a guaranteed procedure.

The paper also connects this work to the Riemann zeta function, the object at the heart of one of mathematics' greatest unsolved problems. Can ζ(s)=0 eventually be stated inside this coinductive framework? That remains open.

This is part of Volume II of an ongoing research series, addressing open problems flagged in Volume I.
