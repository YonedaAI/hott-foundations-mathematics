One of the most celebrated ideas in modern mathematics is this: if two mathematical structures are "equivalent," they should count as THE SAME thing. You should not be able to tell them apart, mathematically speaking.

Homotopy Type Theory makes this precise with something called the Univalence Axiom: equivalent types ARE identical. It is a beautiful, powerful idea — and it is fundamentally symmetric. Equivalences go both ways. Every identity can be reversed.

But most of the really interesting structure in mathematics is NOT symmetric. Categories have arrows that point one direction. A group representation is covariant — it goes from group elements to transformations, not the other way around. The Langlands correspondence — one of the deepest conjectures in modern mathematics — is a directed relationship between two very different kinds of mathematical objects.

If type theory is going to serve as a genuine foundation for all of modern mathematics, it needs a DIRECTED version of univalence. This has been an open problem since 2017.

A new paper surveys the full landscape of this problem, from the original 2017 partial result of Riehl and Shulman through the first complete theorem proved in 2024 by Gratzer, Weinberger, and Buchholtz — which covers only a fragment (the "discrete" types). The paper then formulates four candidate complete principles of increasing strength, and carefully analyses what obstacles stand in the way of proving each one.

The obstacles are real: managing "opposite" directions, dealing with modal logic inside the type theory, and the absence of an internal universe of all categories as first-class objects.

The complete principle remains open. But this paper maps the territory more precisely than ever before — and that is essential groundwork for connecting type theory to the Langlands programme.
