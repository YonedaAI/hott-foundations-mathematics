This is a review of the research paper "Directed Univalence: From Riehl--Shulman to a Complete Principle" by YonedaAI Research.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent and timely expository paper that provides a structured overview of the development of directed univalence, from the partial principles in Riehl-Shulman's Simplicial Type Theory to the complete principle for discrete types in Gratzer-Weinberger-Buchholtz's Triangulated Type Theory. The paper's main contribution is not a new mathematical result but rather the clear articulation of a layered research programme for achieving a complete directed univalence principle for Rezk types and beyond. It successfully identifies the key concepts, theorems, and, crucially, the open problems and technical obstructions. The logical structure is clear, the writing is of high quality, and it serves as a valuable guide for researchers entering this area.

#### **Minor Issues & Suggestions**

1.  **[Clarity] Informal Definitions:**
    *   **Line 312 (Definition 3.6):** The description of the Segal condition refers to the "inner horn of the standard 2-simplex" `\seg^2_1` without a formal definition. While standard for experts, adding a brief parenthetical description (e.g., "the shape generated by `(t,0)` and `(1,s)` in the unit square") or a direct citation to the definition in Riehl-Shulman would improve self-containedness for a broader audience.
    *   **Line 389 (Proof sketch for Thm 5.3):** The term `\mathsf{cofib}(f, t)` is used without explanation. A short descriptive phrase, such as "using a standard cofibrant path-object construction to interpolate between A and B," would clarify the mechanism for readers not intimately familiar with the proof.

2.  **[LaTeX Quality] Whitespace and Layout:**
    *   **Line 132 (Title Block):** The `\author` and `\date` fields are standard. However, the `GrokRxiv` sidebar, while a nice aesthetic touch, is implemented with a hard-coded `xshift` (`38pt`). This may not be robust across different document classes or paper sizes. This is purely cosmetic and does not affect readability.
    *   **Line 550 (Table):** The table summarizing the status of the univalence candidates is excellent. The LaTeX is functional, but using the `booktabs` package would produce a more professional-looking table with better spacing.

3.  **[Completeness] Vague or Future Citations:**
    *   **Line 281 (Bibliography [Riehl2025]):** The reference "[Riehl2025]" cites an arXiv paper from the future (`2510.15795`). This should be replaced with an actual publication, a current arXiv ID if available, or rephrased as "E. Riehl, personal communication" or "in preparation" if that is the case.
    *   **Line 285 (Bibliography [KavvosWeaver]):** The reference "[KavvosWeaver]" is described as "Various preprints, 2024--2025". This is too vague for a formal bibliography. Specific arXiv IDs or publications should be cited. If the work is ongoing and not yet public, it may be better to remove the citation or mention it in the text as "ongoing work by Kavvos and Weaver".

4.  **[Style] Author and Date Conventions:**
    *   **Line 132 (Author):** The author is listed as "YonedaAI Research". While collective authorship is valid, for formal publication, journals often prefer a list of individual contributors.
    *   **Line 134 (Date):** The date is set to "4 May 2026". This should be changed to the actual date of submission.

5.  **[Clarity] Synthetic Representation Theory:**
    *   **Line 606 (Definition 7.3):** The definition of a synthetic representation is excellent. To make the connection to classical theory even clearer, it might be beneficial to add a sentence explaining that the Rezk-type group `G` is presented via its delooping `BG`. For example: "...of a Rezk-type groupoid `G` is a directed morphism `G \to \mathsf{Vect}`... where groups are typically considered as their one-object groupoid deloopings `BG`."
