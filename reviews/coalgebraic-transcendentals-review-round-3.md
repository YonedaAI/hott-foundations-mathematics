This review assesses the paper "Final Coalgebras and Transcendental Numbers in HoTT: A Coinductive Characterisation of $\pi$ and $e$". The evaluation covers mathematical correctness, clarity, completeness, structure, and typesetting quality.

### Overall Summary

The paper presents a compelling and well-motivated alternative to the standard inductive (HIIT) construction of real numbers in HoTT, focusing on a coinductive approach using final coalgebras for streams. The central goal—to provide a purely coalgebraic characterisation of transcendental numbers like $\pi$ and $e$—is ambitious and highly relevant. The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and demonstrates a strong command of the subject matter. However, there are significant issues with the framing of the main contribution and a critical inconsistency regarding the claimed formalisation, which prevent a recommendation for publication in its current form.

---

### Critical Issues

1.  **Contradictory Claims about Formalisation:** The conclusion (lines 514-516) states, "The framework is implemented in Haskell ... and in Lean 4/Mathlib using the `Stream'` API." However, Open Problem 8.3 (line 474) explicitly lists "Formalise productivity of the BBP and Sale digit-extraction algorithms in Lean 4/Mathlib" as future work. A proof of productivity is essential for the corecursive definitions (`unfold`) to be total, meaning a key part of the claimed Lean implementation is admittedly incomplete. This is a major contradiction that undermines the paper's claims of having a complete and verified framework. The author must clarify the exact status of the formalisation.

### Major Revisions

1.  **Overstated "Internal" Characterisation:** The paper's primary contribution is presented as the "internal" characterisations of $\pi$ and $e$ (Theorems 5.8 and 5.9), which claim to remove the dependency on the Cauchy reals (`Rc`). However, the proof sketches rely on a `state` retraction (clarified in Appendix B) which is only defined for streams already known to be generated by the given algorithm. This makes the characterisation appear tautological: "The unique stream satisfying the corecursive equation for algorithm X is the stream generated by algorithm X." This does not, on its own, uniquely identify the number $\pi$ or $e$ in a self-contained way. The paper acknowledges this "internalisation gap" in Problem 8.1, but this limitation should be discussed transparently alongside the main theorems themselves, not postponed until the "Open Problems" section. The contribution in Section 5.3 should be re-framed to more accurately reflect what has been achieved: a formalisation of spigot algorithms as unique solutions to corecursive equations within the stream coalgebra, while noting that proving these streams correspond to the classical numbers *without* recourse to an external structure (`Rc`) remains an open challenge.

2.  **Insufficient Detail in Core Proofs:** The proofs for the main internalisation theorems (5.8 and 5.9) are too brief. Given the subtlety of the "up-to-context" coinduction argument and the central importance of these claims, a more detailed walkthrough is necessary. Specifically, the definition and properties of the `state` function and the compatibility of the `up-to` operator (sketched in Appendix B) should be integrated more clearly into the main proof argument to make it self-contained and convincing.

### Minor Revisions

1.  **Clarity on Bisimulation Proofs:** The proof of Proposition 5.11 (showing that streams from Leibniz, Machin, and BBP are bisimilar) is correct but relies entirely on mapping to `Rc` via `alpha_b`. The following remark rightly points out this limitation. To strengthen the coinductive narrative, the author could briefly speculate on what a direct, internal bisimulation proof between two different algorithms (e.g., Machin and Leibniz) might entail, perhaps by constructing a state machine that runs both algorithms in parallel and relates their intermediate states. This would better motivate Problem 8.1.
2.  **Citation Style:** The paper cites a fictional series of prior papers by the same author (e.g., `[1]`, `[2]`, `[3]`). While Appendix A commendably makes the paper self-contained by restating the necessary theorems, this citation practice is non-standard. The author should integrate these prerequisites more naturally, for example by stating "We rely on the following established theorems in cubical HoTT, adapted from Booij [4] and Ahrens et al. [7]..."
3.  **LaTeX and Typos:**
    *   Line 28: A space is missing in `GrokRxiv:2026.05.coalgebraic-transcendentals`. It should probably be `2026.05`.
    *   Line 124: "HIIT path-constructor" should likely be plural: "path-constructors".
    *   Line 227: "definable as a coalgebra morphism" -> "definable as coalgebra morphisms".
    *   Line 317: The sentence beginning "The propositional truncation..." is slightly awkward. Suggestion: "Since the fibers of the quotient map are propositions (a consequence of working with h-sets), the total space is a proposition. Combined with inhabitation, this proves contractibility."
    *   Line 441: `mutatis mutandis` is a standard academic phrase, but simply "largely apply" would be clearer for a broader audience.

### Verdict

The paper tackles an important problem with a creative and well-informed approach. The comparison between inductive and coinductive presentations of the reals is illuminating. However, the overstatement of the main result and the critical inconsistency regarding the formal verification work are serious flaws that must be addressed.
